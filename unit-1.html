1.1 What is discourse?

Before moving on to discourse analysis, we need to understand what discourse refers to. However, this concept is very complex to define, so the first thing to bear in mind is that usually a definition of discourse is attached to the author’s method or approach to analyze it. To begin with, we will address discourse from general perspectives, so let’s look into the following authors’ conceptions.

Discourse definition

Instrucción: Press the buttons to access the information.

Gee (1996)

For Gee (1996), Discourse (with a capital D) is “a socially accepted association among ways of using language, other symbolic expressions, and ‘artifacts,’ of thinking, feeling, believing, valuing, and acting that can be used to identify oneself as a member of a socially meaningful group or ‘social network,’ or to signal (that one is playing) a socially meaningful ‘role’” (p.131). In other words, discourse should be conceived as a social practice embracing not only what can be done with the language in real life but also mental and emotional elements that influence these oral interactions.

Thornbury (2005)

Put more simply, Thornbury (2005, p. 7) states that “discourse is the way that language – either spoken or written – is used for communicative effect in a real-world situation”. Hence, anything that can be said or written to communicate intended meanings can be considered discourse, highlighting its functional and purposeful use in authentic contexts to achieve a specific goal or reaction, which is its core pragmatic function.

Alsaawi (2016)

Alsaawi (2016, p. 19), entails tangible examples of what discourse can ‘look like’: “discourse can be either written, such as in books, essays, newspapers, magazines, road signs or invoices, or spoken, such as in conversations, verbal interactions and TV programmes”. This perspective primarily focuses on the observable products and physical manifestations of language, categorizing them by their modality to provide a concrete and accessible understanding of the linguistic phenomenon in everyday life, listing its common forms and mediums.

As you can see, the term discourse involves two main mediums, spoken and written, and one main hallmark, society. Therefore, the way discourse interacts with people is through its mediums (we can also add digital discourse – see Crystal, 2013 – which contains characteristics of both and more) and the element that influences them is the social interactions in a specific context.

What is Discourse?
Communication involves much more than just forming isolated sentences. It is a process where language is used in a complete and purposeful way. In linguistics, this is called discourse, which is defined as a unit of language that is longer than a single sentence and is used in a real-life situation.

While grammar deals with small elements like sounds (phonemes) or parts of words (morphemes), discourse analysis studies how language—wFhether spoken or written—is used in a social setting. This discipline, significantly advanced by the work of linguist Teun van Dijk, examines how larger units of language—such as words, grammar, and crucially, context—combine to give meaning to a conversation or a text.

The central idea is that discourse is entirely dependent on context. Real communication goes beyond the words that are spoken; it includes situational knowledge and background information shared by the speaker and the listener. Therefore, meaning cannot be deduced solely from the words uttered but requires an understanding of the circumstances surrounding the communicative act.

In summary, discourse is the study of language in action, analyzing how it is structured and interpreted in practice, always considering the situation in which it occurs to achieve effective communication.

Spoken discourse

Spoken discourse refers to language that is produced and understood in real-time communicative exchanges. It is usually interactive, dynamic, and influenced by context. It contains features such as turn-taking, pauses, and fillers (e.g., "uh," "you know"), among others. Unlike written discourse, it relies on intonation, body language, and shared knowledge between speakers to convey meaning. As Cornish (2006) mentions: “[it] is the ongoing, situated interpretation of a speaker’s communicative intentions, of which the addressee’s expected and actual reactions are an integral part” (p. 1).

To analyze spoken discourse, we must consider its most prominent characteristics, which are often “related to phonetic (e.g. voicing, pauses) and phonological (e.g. intonation, phonemic differences) features. On the other hand, certain grammatical, lexical and pragmatic features have also been specifically associated with spoken discourse and can therefore not be properly analysed or investigated without the inclusion of spoken data in the analysis” (Ghanem et al., 2024).

Some examples of spoken discourse with distinctive features include:

  • Telephone calls (business and private).
  • Service encounters (shops, ticket offices, etc.).
  • Interviews (jobs, journalistic, in official settings).
  • Classroom (classes, seminars, lectures, tutorials).
  • Rituals (church prayers, sermons, weddings).
  • Monologues (speeches, stories, jokes).
  • Language-in-action (talk accompanying doing, such as fixing, cooking, assembling, demonstrating, etc.).
  • Casual conversation (strangers, friends, intimates).
  • Organizing and directing people (work, home, in the street).

(McCarthy, 1991, pp. 118-119)

As we will discuss in later Units, there are several techniques to perform discourse analysis on spoken interactions. Some of them are studying turn-taking, analyzing adjacency pairs, exploring repair, identifying discourse markers, among others. For now, consider the following example:

A conversation between two coworkers:

  • Randy: Hey, did you finish the report?
  • Martha: Uh… not yet. I need, like, a few more hours.
  • Randy: Oh, no worries. The deadline’s tomorrow, right?
  • Martha: Yeah, but I wanna make sure it’s good, you know?
  • Randy: Totally. Let me know if you need help.
  • Martha: Thanks, I appreciate it!


This is a transcribed conversation that occurred in an office building some months ago. What features can you identify that are specific for spoken discourse?

For one, the register is informal. People use fillers like “Uh”, “like”, “you know”, among others, in the flow of the conversation while they think of what to say and how to say it, which is natural. Secondly, the sentences are shorter than they would be, say, if you were writing an email. Moreover, as it is implied from reading the talk, you can guess there are pauses in between the utterances, to make interaction natural. Finally, there are several contractions, which are also characteristic of spoken, casual language.

Written discourse

On the other hand, written discourse refers to language that is structured and organized in writing and has a clear purpose and audience in mind. It typically follows grammatical conventions, makes use of different cohesive devices (connectors, pronouns), and has a more planned structure compared to spoken discourse. Moreover, it lacks immediate interaction and relies on textual features to convey meaning effectively. As Cameron and Panovic (2014) mention, “written language is not acquired ‘naturally’. Individuals need to be taught to read and write, and many societies have managed perfectly well without any knowledge of the written world” (Cameron and Panovic, 2014).

“Written discourse is considered an imperative aspect that needs to be analysed. Cohesion, coherence, clause relations and text patterns are all parts of written discourse (Alsaawi, 2016)”.

In this instance of written discourse, you can see some key differences with the spoken discourse. First, the text has a formal structure (i.e., greeting, body, closing remark, etc.). The sentences are also longer and complete because there was some thought put into them before committing them to writing. Furthermore, the vocabulary used is more complex and advanced, there are no contractions because of the formal register, which also means there is no real-time interaction happening.

Paltridge (2012)

Paltridge (2012)

Discourse analysis examines patterns of language across texts and considers the relationship between language and the social and cultural contexts in which it is used. Discourse analysis also considers the ways that the use of language presents different views of the world and different understandings. It examines how the use of language is influenced by relationships between participants as well as the effects the use of language has upon social identities and relations. It also considers how views of the world, and identities, are constructed through the use of discourse.

Alsaawi (2016)

Alsaawi (2016)

Discourse analysis is the study of language in either spoken or written form. It moves beyond the level of isolated sentences or words to explore how language is structured and used in real-world communication. This involves examining longer stretches of text or conversation to understand how coherence and meaning are achieved. Ultimately, it aims to uncover the rules and conventions that govern how we use language to create meaningful exchanges.

Discourse analysis and English Language Teaching (ELT)


In relation to ELT, Rymes (2008) has defined discourse analysis as “the study of how language-in-use is affected by the context of its use. In the classroom, context can range from the talk within a lesson, to a student’s entire lifetime of socialization, to the history of the institution of schooling” (p.12).

Discourse Analysis (DA) examines language use and its relationship with the sociocultural context so as to understand how people convey meaning through it. In other words, it studies the relation between language and reality in order to describe and analyse the words that people use in spoken and written interactions (Jacobs & Tschötschel, 2019; Paltridge, 2012). In this sense, Paltridge and Wang (2015) point out that DA focuses on analysing language aspects such as:

  • Language patterns which occur during the production of written and spoken texts.
  • Language knowledge at different levels, beyond word, phrase, clause, and sentence.
  • What people meant to say and how they conveyed meaning.
  • The relationship between language use and its socio-cultural context.
  • The way in which any language builds different perspectives and meanings of the world.

In this course, we will more closely address discourse used by the people involved in the academic context such as teacher-students’ interactions within classrooms, schools and institutions whether in spoken or written discourse.
